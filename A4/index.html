Proposition: States that restrict abortion clinic access don't reduce abortions, but instead they redirect them, pushing residents to cross state lines while turning neighboring states into regional hubs.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Annotating Missouri, Wyoming, and South Dakota with exact travel percentages (Score: +1.5). Calling out the three most extreme states by name with precise numbers (99%, 88%, 84%) makes the pattern feel concrete and data-driven. It directs the reader's eye to the strongest evidence for the proposition. The main earnestness caveat is that these three are outliers, and labeling only them implicitly suggests they're representative of a broader pattern when most states cluster much lower.
  • Red color scale for out-of-state travel (Score: -0.5). Red carries connotations of danger or alarm, priming readers to interpret high out-of-state travel as a problem before they've formed their own judgment. A neutral sequential scale (e.g. light to dark blue) would be more objective. This was a deliberate choice to trigger an emotional response aligned with the proposition.
  • Dual encoding: fill color + circle size (Score: +1). Using two encodings simultaneously, state color for displacement, circle size for abortions performed, lets readers see both sides of the story on one map. States that are light-colored (low travel) with large circles visually read as "destination states," which directly supports the displacement narrative. It's an earnest choice in that both variables are faithfully represented, though the combination steers interpretation.
  • Title framing: "Restrictions Don't Stop Abortions — They Export Them" (Score: -1). The title asserts the proposition's conclusion as established fact rather than a hypothesis the visualization is exploring. This is one of the most persuasive elements because readers often absorb titles uncritically. An earnest alternative would be something descriptive like "Out-of-State Abortion Travel by State, 2020." The assertive framing was a deliberate tradeoff between persuasiveness and neutrality.
  • Underlying policy context from Guttmacher's interactive policy map (Score: +2) Cross-referencing the visualization against Guttmacher's state policy tracker confirms that Missouri, Wyoming, and South Dakota are among the most restrictive states in the country, validating that the out-of-state travel pattern aligns with policy restrictiveness rather than being coincidental. This is a fully earnest decision, grounding the visual pattern in an independent policy dataset strengthens the causal argument and is transparently verifiable by any reader.

AGAINST the Proposition

Final Reflection

Going into this assignment I assumed the "deceptive" visualization would feel obviously wrong to make, but it actually didn't, and that was the surprising part. Most of the choices that made Visualization 2 misleading were things I could easily rationalize: excluding Missouri because it's extreme, truncating the axis because the chart "looks cleaner," framing the title assertively because that's just good communication. None of it felt like lying in the moment, which made me realize how easy it is to deceive an audience while convincing yourself you're being reasonable.

The hardest part was drawing a line between persuasion and manipulation. Both visualizations make argumentative choices, like the red color scale in Visualization 1, the annotation calling out restrictive states in Visualization 2, but some feel more defensible than others. I think the closest thing to a workable definition of ethical visualization is whether you'd be comfortable explaining every decision out loud to a skeptical reader. The Missouri exclusion in Visualization 2 doesn't pass that test, and I'd have to admit I removed it because it hurt my argument, not because it was genuinely an outlier. That feels like the real boundary, not whether a choice is technically accurate, but whether your reasoning for it holds up under scrutiny.
