Overall Analysis Questions

  • Is there a correlation between class zoning and fiscal zoning? Motivation: One of the readings mentioned class zoning as a cause of social hierarchy and fiscal zoning as a cause of differences in economic class. From what I’ve seen and heard, I feel like there should be correlation between these since it would be reasonable for ones who make more money to have higher tax revenues.
  • Have suburbs tried to welcome those who were excluded since the 1970s when they wanted to preserve “status-quo”? Motivation: In my experience, suburbs, especially wealthy ones, still primarily exist of white people. How did we recover from the big downzoning?
  • In what way does Boston’s progressive political identity conflict with the lived reality of segregation? Motivation: Boston is often seen as liberal and educated, yet it is still deeply segregated. I want to understand whether commitments to equity differ from outcomes, and why if that gap persists.

Discoveries and Insights

Summary & Conclusion

Through this analysis, it became clear that historical class zoning, as reflected in the HOLC grades, continues to have a lasting impact on economic and racial segregation in the Greater Boston area. The A/B (Best and Still Desirable) zones historically attracted wealthier, predominantly white populations, while C/D (Definitely Declining and Hazardous) areas were often home to lower-income and marginalized communities, including Black, immigrant, and Jewish populations. This division in zoning practices reinforced economic and racial hierarchies, influencing property values and tax revenues in different neighborhoods.

The visualization of HOLC grades further highlighted the persistence of segregation in liberal cities like Boston and Chelsea, where significant portions of the population live in C/D zones despite the progressive political identity of these areas. These cities, while advocating for equity and inclusion, still exhibit clear divisions in wealth and opportunity that are rooted in their historical zoning practices. In contrast, more evenly split political cities, such as Brookline and Saugus, show a more mixed distribution of A/B vs C/D zones, suggesting that political identity may not always be directly aligned with racial and economic integration.

Additionally, while Greater Boston has seen an increase in racial diversity since the 1970s, particularly in suburban areas, the pace of this integration has been slow and uneven. Factors such as housing policies, zoning laws, and economic barriers have continued to limit the full integration of previously excluded populations into suburban communities. Despite some progress, the legacy of segregation remains a deep-rooted challenge in the region, demonstrating that commitments to equity at the political level do not always lead to immediate, tangible outcomes for marginalized communities.

In conclusion, the analysis of HOLC grades and the distribution of wealth and racial composition in Greater Boston provides a strong visual and statistical case for the lasting effects of class zoning. It also highlights the disconnect between progressive political identities and the economic disparities that continue to shape Boston’s neighborhoods.
